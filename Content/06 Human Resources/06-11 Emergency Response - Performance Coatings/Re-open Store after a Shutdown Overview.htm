Re-open Store after a Shutdown Overview

After any site shutdown, we want to get back to normal, and back to business, as safely and as soon as possible.

Here are some tips to help you plan for the aftermath of a shutdown:

  • Identify other S-W locations that can serve your customers.
  • Have a list of key customers saved in your phone to stay in contact.
  • Forward the facility phone to your cell phone, if possible.

Review the following EHS Considerations with Employees or Contractors prior to doing the Cleanup Work:

Hazard Risks Associated Protective Measures
Contaminated water, surfaces, & product
  • Bacteria and other infectiousorganisms (from sewage) in water, soil, & residue.
  • Toxic substances from flooded industrial & waste sites and spilled hazardous product.
  • Mold in the air.
  • Ventilate enclosed spaces with fresh air.
  • Assume that floodwater, surfaces, and product are contaminated if they got wet. Discard all.
  • Allow only trained workers with the proper personal protective equipment to clean up toxic chemicals, other hazardous waste, & mold.
  • Be up-to-date with tetanus shot (last 10 years).
  • Use waterproof boots, latex or rubber gloves and other protective clothing. Consider using special chemical resistant (Tyvek or Kleenguard) outer clothing and protective goggles.
  • Use an N-95 NIOSH-approved disposable respirator, at a minimum, when handling mold-contaminated materials.
  • Keep an adequate supply of clean water available for drinking and washing.
  • Use hand sanitizer and sanitize or wash hands before eating/drinking/smoking. There is no eating/drinking/smoking in the store until cleared.
Downed power lines, gas leaks & other electrical hazards
  • Burns/electrocution from contact with energized lines or objects, including tree limbs touching downed power lines.
  • Fire & explosion from natural gas leaks.
  • Burns & electrocution from contact with impaired/shorted equipment (ie: shakers, tinters, powered pallet jacks batteries, and battery chargers) or building electrical outlets.
  • Assume that all power lines are energized.
  • Establish and clearly mark a danger zone around downed lines and stay at least 10 feet away.
  • Allow only properly trained and equipped workers to repair electrical wires.
  • Report any suspected gas leaks and leave the building promptly.
  • Do not use powered equipment or tools until the building electrical is deemed safe for use.
Falls from elevated heights and slips/trips/ falls
  • Falls from ladders, roofs, & other elevated work surfaces.
  • Slippery & uneven working surfaces that can create injuries due to slips, trips, & falls.
  • Do not go on the facility roof.
  • Use proper ladder safety (e.g., set on firm & stable ground, maintain “three-point” contact, etc.)
  • Ensure there are adequate paths to exit all spaces of the store.
  • Be aware of wet or slippery surfaces, obstacles, or uneven surfaces on the site.
  • Move and work slowly and methodically, and exercise good spatial awareness (look before you walk, be aware of overhead hazards, etc.).
If the facility uses a generator
  • Shocks and electrocution from gas and diesel powered generators.
  • Toxic carbon monoxide (CO) from generator exhaust.
  • Fires from improper refueling and fuel storage.
  • Never run a portable generator inside the building, warehouse, or in an enclosed space.
  • Inspect electric cords to ensure they are free of defects. Use a ground-fault circuit interrupter.
  • Shut down the generator before refueling. Never store fuel or the generator indoors.
Construction activities and clean-up work at the facility
  • High noise from tools and generators.
  • Chemicals used for clean-up
  • Exposure to asbestos-contaminated materials during the demolition and clean-up work.
  • If available, use hearing protection when working with loud tools or around generators.
  • Properly select and use PPE (see Emergency Cleanup Procedure or contact Divisional/Corp. EHS).
  • Do not use solvents for cleaning.
Ergonomic injuries
  • Risk of back, knee, & shoulder injuries from manual lifting and handling of building materials and fallen tree limbs.
  • Exercise good lifting techniques.
  • Make two-person or team lifts for heavy objects.
  • Know your limitations. Don’t be in a hurry.
