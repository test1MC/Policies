Email Etiquette Overview

Sherwin-Williams’ The Americas Group email etiquette rules were implemented for the following reasons:

  • Professionalism: Using proper language will convey a professional image.
  • Efficiency: Emails that get to the point are much more effective than poorly worded emails.
  • Protection from Liability: Awareness of email risks will protect the Company from costly lawsuits.
Guideline Description

Be Concise

Do not make an email longer than it needs to be. Remember that reading an email is harder than reading printed communications and long emails can be very discouraging to read.

Answer and Anticipate Questions

An email reply must answer all questions and pre-empt further questions. If all of the customer's questions in the original email are not answered, the customer will have to send a follow-up email which wastes time for store employees and the customer. Moreover, if able to pre-empt relevant questions, the customer will be grateful for the efficient and thoughtful customer service.

Use Proper Spelling, Grammar, and Punctuation

Improper spelling, grammar, and punctuation give a bad impression of the Company and allow for miscommunications. Emails with no full stops or commas are difficult to read and can sometimes change the meaning of the text. Always use the spell check option on the email program, if available.

Make It Personal

Not only should the email be personally addressed, it should also include customized content. Auto replies are usually not very effective because they are impersonal.

Answer Swiftly

Customers send emails because they wish to receive quick responses. Each email should be replied to within at least 24 hours and preferably within the same working day. If the inquiry is complicated and will require some time to answer, reply by confirming that the store has received it and that a reply will be sent as soon as possible.

Do Not Attach Unnecessary Files

Sending unnecessary attachments can annoy customers and slow down their email systems. Make sure that to get permission before sending a customer an attached file. Emails are routed through the Sherwin-Williams’ Company Intranet to eliminate viruses and monitor file size.

Use Proper Structure and Layout

Since reading from a screen is more difficult than reading from paper, an email’s structure and layout is very important. Use short paragraphs and blank lines between each paragraph. When making numerous points, separate them with numbers, letters, or bullets.

DO NOT WRITE IN CAPITALS (Also Known as “ALL CAPS”)

IF YOU WRITE IN CAPITALS, IT SEEMS AS IF YOU ARE SHOUTING. This can be highly annoying.

Don’t Leave Out the Message Thread

When replying to an email, include the original message in the reply. Do this by clicking Reply rather than creating a new message. Leaving the thread might take a fraction longer to download, but it will save the recipient from needing to search for the original message.

Read the Email Before You Send It

Read an email before sending it to avoid spelling and grammar mistakes contained in emails. Try to read your message through the eyes of the recipient. This will help to send more effective emails and avoid misunderstandings and inappropriate comments.

Do Not Overuse ‘Reply to All’

Only use Reply to All if needing the message to be seen by each person who received the original message.

Be Mindful of Abbreviations

In business emails, try not to use abbreviations or emojis such as BTW (by the way), LOL (laugh out loud), and the smiley face :-). The recipient might not be aware of the abbreviation’s meaning and, generally, these are not appropriate in business emails. If unsure whether the recipient knows what an abbreviation means, it’s best not to use it.

Be Careful with Formatting

Remember that formatting can appear differently for senders and recipients. Whenever possible, create basic email messages to ensure that the receiver will be able to view and understand the message.

Do Not Forward Chain Letters

Chain letters are hoaxes. Delete them as soon as they are received.

Do Not Copy a Message or Attachment Without Permission

Get permission before copying a message or attachment to avoid infringing on copyright laws.

Do Not Use Email to Discuss Confidential Information

Sending an email is like sending a postcard. Don't send the email if you would not want it displayed on a bulletin board. Moreover, never make any libelous, sexist, or racially discriminating comments in emails, even if they are meant to be a joke.

Use a Meaningful Subject

Try to use a subject that is meaningful to the recipient as well as yourself. For instance, when sending an email to a company requesting information about a product, it is better to mention the actual name of the product. For example, title a subject 'Product [A] Information' rather than 'product information' or the company's name.

Use Active Voice

Whenever possible, use the active voice of a verb. For instance, 'We will process your order today,' sounds better than, 'Your order will be processed today.' The former sounds more personal, whereas the latter, especially when used frequently, sounds unnecessarily formal.

Avoid Using URGENT and IMPORTANT

Avoid these types of exclamations at all times, unless the email actually warrants them.

Avoid Long Sentences

Try to keep your sentences to a maximum of 15-20 words. Email is meant to be a quick medium and requires a different kind of writing than letters. If someone receives an email that looks like a dissertation, chances are that they will not even attempt to read it.

Do Not Send or Forward Emails Containing Defamatory, Offensive, Racist, or Obscene Remarks

By sending or even forwarding one defamatory or offensive remark in an email, you and the Company can face court cases resulting in multi-million dollar penalties.

Do Not Forward Virus Hoaxes

If you receive an email message warning you of a new unstoppable virus that will immediately delete everything from your computer, this is most probably a hoax. By forwarding hoaxes you use valuable bandwidth, and sometimes virus hoaxes contain viruses themselves. The same goes for chain letters that promise incredible riches or ask for help for a charitable cause. Even if the content seems to be bona fide, the senders are usually not. Since it is impossible to find out whether a chain letter is real or not, the best place for it is the recycle bin. If you encounter these types of messages, notify Store Computer Operations at 216-566-2740.

Keep Your Language Gender Neutral

Avoid using sexist language such as, 'The user should add a signature by configuring his email program.' Be inclusive by using ‘him/her,’ or simply construct your sentence as gender neutral: ‘The user should add a signature by configuring the email program.’

Do Not Reply to Spam

You confirm that your email address is live when you reply to spam or unsubscribe from a mailing list, which generates even more spam. Therefore, just hit the delete button.

Attach Proper Salutations

If you normally address a person as Miss/Mrs./Ms./Mr. Smith, then this is how you should initially address your recipient in an email. If you normally call your recipients by their first names, you might want to address the email accordingly. When in doubt, stick to the formal salutation.

Include Signatures

Since you will not be able to sign your emails, include an electronic signature at the bottom of your email messages. This ensures that the recipient will understand who originated the email. Here is an example of an electronic signature:

John Smith
Store Manager
The Sherwin-Williams Company
SW1234@sherwin.com
