Colorant Characterization Overview

This section does not apply to Automotive Branches or General Polymers

Colorants work modestly different in each product base. For example, the L1 [blue] colorant produces different results in Extra White base by extra white base across different product lines.

Colorant Characterization is the process of determining exactly how our colorants will act in each and every base into which we are going to dispense. The characterization process involves making very precise compounds [accurate to 1 part in 100,000]. Each colorant is used in either 6 or 10 panels to tell the system exactly how the colorant reacts in that base. Six combinations are made for Extra White base or Luminous Base and 10 combinations are made for deep or ultra deep bases.

Opaque stains [Woodscape Solid Stains] will be characterized. For now, semi-transparent stains will not be characterized because the substrate shows through the semi-transparent stains. We can predict the color of the semi-transparent stain over a naturally light or naturally dark finish, but we cannot predict how the semi-transparent color is going to appear over a variety of grains and inherent colors.
