Store Safety Overview

Name Protocol
Sales Floor

A clean, orderly, and well-merchandised store is more attractive to customers and provides a safer working environment for employees. Creating and maintaining a safe store involves:

  • Identifying and correcting hazards.
  • Stocking merchandise displays properly.
  • Using electrical equipment safely.
  • Safe handling and cleanup of paint products.
  • Properly using and maintaining store equipment, including motorized vehicles.

Note: Refer to the LiveSafe manual found on the Safety tab on SOURCE and the related START Modules for more information on facility maintenance.
Sales Floor Gondolas
  • Merchandise must be displayed "facing off" on the shelves. This means that items should be placed right up to the edges and corners of each shelf. Even if current stock is low and there is not enough to fill the shelf, place available stock items at the front of the shelf. Any vacant space will then be behind the merchandise, not in front of it. This prevents customers and employees from tearing their clothing or getting cut reaching for items at the back of the display.
Ladder
  • All sales floor display ladders must be stored in a rack and held in place with a security chain. This prevents ladders from falling on someone.
  • For information on ladder usage, refer to the Safety & Health START Modules.
Merchandise Display
  • All store merchandise displays should be stocked properly to avoid injuries. For instance, someone may accidentally knock over items that are loosely stacked or trip over articles that are left in the aisles. To prevent these types of injuries, keep store displays stocked neatly and safely.
  • For additional information, refer to chapter 3 in the LiveSafe Manual.
Paint Can Display
  • Floor Paint Can displays must be stacked securely so they will not fall over. For a stable display, the cans must be arranged in a staggered formation, not stacked in columns.
  • Floor paint displays should not be stacked over four cans high.
Pegboard
  • To prevent eye injury, all pegs must be stocked with merchandise. If there are no items hung on a peg, remove it from the board. Pegboards should also have display shelves below them so a customer cannot stand close to the pegboard.
Stacking Sherwin-Williams has specific guidelines for stacking stock items in the warehouse. Proper stacking reduces the risk of personal injury. Any warehouse material that is stacked on the floor and must be handpicked should not be stacked higher than five feet.
  • Metal five-gallon pails must be piled securely, and the stack cannot be over four and one-half feet high.
  • Plastic five-gallon containers cannot be stacked over four and one-half feet.
  • Gallon paint can cases or cartons cannot be stacked higher than four and one-half feet high. Individual paint cans must not be stacked in a column.
  • Paint Stackers for gallon cans are available from Order Express. (7 Circle #4000218 and 4 Diamond #4000219)
  • Any product that is on a broken pallet should be placed on a good pallet before being moved or put into storage.
